Not sure what you should be doing with your money? Your age plays a big role in the types of financial decisions you should be making.

Click the tabs below to make sure you’re spending and saving wisely.

Retirement

In your 20s
The earlier you start saving, the better. Open your first retirement account – either a 401K through your employer or an IRA if you’re self-employed.

In your 30s
As your salary grows, so should your retirement contributions. Aim to put away at least 15% of your income toward the future.

In your 40s
Meet with a financial planner to make sure you’re on track for retirement. Increases in your contributions now could have a big payoff later on. Avoid dipping into your retirement savings to fund present expenses or splurges and make sure retirement is taken care of before funding a child’s college education (there are no loans for retirement!).

For additional help, speak with a financial planner who can show you where to begin on your road to financial security.

Credit Cards

In your 20s
Limit yourself to two good cards to use (one as a backup) and never miss a payment. Avoid store cards, which tend to have higher interest rates.

In your 30s
Check your credit score regularly on sites like CreditKarma.com and AnnualCreditReport.com. Ideally, you should keep your balances under 30% for good credit health. It will show lenders that you use credit responsibly and that you don’t rely too heavily on borrowed money.

In your 40s
As long as you can pay off your balances in full, go ahead & maximize your cash back, travel points, and other rewards.

For additional help, speak with a financial planner who can show you where to begin on your road to financial security.

Emergency Fund

In your 20s
You never know when you’ll need it. Aim to have a minimum of six months’ expenses put aside. You can save this over time by making automatic contributions from your paychecks.

In your 30s
Once family, home ownership, and other factors come into play, it makes sense to create more of a financial cushion in case of emergency. Try to increase your savings in this category over time.

In your 40s
Emergencies can happen at any time, but as you get a little older, you should think about the prospect of potential health issues or other types of unexpected expenses you may not have anticipated. Make sure your total emergency fund increases in line with your current income, which will likely go up after your 20s or 30s.

For additional help, speak with a financial planner who can show you where to begin on your road to financial security.

Real Estate

In your 20s
Use a rent vs. own calculator to see whether buying makes more sense for you. Start saving up for a down payment if you hope to buy.

In your 30s
Before buying a home, it is generally recommend that buyers save up for a 20% down payment.

In your 40s
Talk to your planner about potentially paying down your mortgage vs. saving for retirement, which could create extra room in your retirement budget and reduce your monthly expenses once you’re no longer working.

For additional help, speak with a financial planner who can show you where to begin on your road to financial security.

Debt

In your 20s
If you have student loans, research repayment options to find one that fits your income. To avoid credit card debt, create a budget that accounts for unexpected expenses.

In your 30s
Don’t let increased responsibilities like a home and expanding family push you into debt by spending more than you make. Aim to get rid of all non-mortgage debt by the end of this decade.

In your 40s
Consider revisiting your budget to make room for retirement savings and your lifestyle. If adjustments are necessary, now is a great time to tackle those.

For additional help, speak with a financial planner who can show you where to begin on your road to financial security.

Learn More

Insurance

In your 20s
Renters, auto, and health insurance is typically the minimum insurance coverage needed in your 20s. Note that you can typically stay on your parent’s health insurance plan until you’re 26, which can help you save money compared to an individual plan.

In your 30s
When you begin to start a family, buy a house, or undertake other ventures that require some risk, it’s a good idea to expand your insurance coverage as these life events occur.

In your 40s
As you get older, it's important to make sure your family is taken care of in case something should happen to you. Putting additional insurance policies in place can help give you peace of mind that your assets will go towards taking care of the ones you love.

For additional help, speak with a financial planner who can show you where to begin on your road to financial security.

EVERYONE IS ON A UNIQUE FINANCIAL JOURNEY

While these financial milestones are certainly something to aspire to, many people aren't able to hit them all decade after decade. If you're a little late to the game, don't worry. It's never too late to start getting your finances in order.

Final Financial Tips:

Start as early as possible when saving for retirement. Always take advantage of employer matching when available.

Tackle your highest interest rate credit cards first. If you’re having trouble paying down credit card debt, switch to cash and debit only.

Open a no-fee online high-yield savings account for your emergency fund. You’ll earn more interest and keep your cash out of sight and out of mind until you need it.

If possible, save 20% for a down payment plus 3% for closing costs. On top of your mortgage principal, you can expect to pay mortgage interest, taxes and insurance.

Be careful about refinancing student loans since you could lose access to forgiveness and repayment options. Once one loan or debt is paid off, add the monthly payment to another debt to reduce overall debt faster. Understand that there is good debt (student loans, mortgage, etc.) and bad debt (auto loans, credit cards, etc.).

In addition to the right types of insurance, make sure you have the appropriate documents set up in case of emergency, such as: a living will, medical power of attorney, beneficiary forms, transfer on death (TOD), payable on death (POD), a will and a trust.
